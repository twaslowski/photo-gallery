With the heatwave continuing its firm grip on central Europe, I'm traveling through Denmark and Germany
for a wedding and an impossible sports event.

<!--more-->

{{< img src="2026/06/europemaxxing/cph-hafen-rutsche.avif" alt="An improvised waterslide around the Copenhagen harbour" >}}

As I already indicated in my last post, the last week of June is a turbulent one for me.
I handed in my laptop at my previous job last Wednesday before starting my new one July 1st.
The one week I have between jobs is spent traveling: From Hamburg to Copenhagen, to celebrate with my
freshly-married friends; back to Hamburg, to pack and grab a good night's sleep; on to Frankfurt,
to cheer on another friend who's taking part in a long-awaited Ironman.

While all of this is happening, temperatures in Germany are reaching record-highs. In some places,
we're beating 40ºC, temperatures unheard of for June. And neither Germany nor Denmark have the infrastructure
to deal with these temperatures: in all countries (that I have traveled to) with this kind of weather,
you get air conditioning everywhere you go, and cold bottled water costs around 50 cents per liter at any
kind of shop. What's more, it is currently midsummer, and the harsh sunlight is ever-present for over fifteen
hours a day, from around 6 in the morning all the way until around 9 in the evening.
There is no respite from the sun, and the concrete never quite cools down.

Still, people try to live their lives the best they can. In Copenhagen specifically, I was incredibly impressed
with the degree of work-life balance that enabled people to hang out in the parks or go for a swim in the
harbor area in the middle of the day. I've tried to capture, as best as I can, the spirit of people making the most of 
the current situation.
